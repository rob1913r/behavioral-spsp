\begin{abstract}
The management of development activities in software projects can be a complex
tactical challenge, where the allocation of tasks to human resources can become
a bottleneck. Task allocation in software projects is traditionally modeled
considering only technical constraints. This paper proposes a Mixed-Integer
Linear Programming (MILP) model for the Software Project Scheduling Problem (SPSP)
that incorporates behavioral compatibility. The formulation penalizes misalignment
through a cubic polynomial function of Solides profiles, allied with a technical
bonus. Solving 1380 instances in the Gurobi Optimizer demonstrates that cognitive
noise increases the makespan and mathematically validates Brooks's Law, providing
a relevant metric for the project's tactical planning.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction} 
 
The software development industry has consolidated itself as one of the main 
pillars of the modern global economy, characterized by rapid delivery cycles 
and dynamic requirements. Unlike traditional manufacturing environments, 
where machine processing is deterministic, the software life cycle is 
fundamentally dependent on human capital. Efficiently managing these resources 
is a challenge in software factories, where professional allocation and 
architectural code dependencies affect the project schedule \citep{dikert2016, faria2022}.

In Operations Research literature, this problem is formalized as the Software 
Project Scheduling Problem (SPSP), a specialized variant of the classic 
\textit{Resource-Constrained Project Scheduling Problem} (RCPSP). Given the 
combinatorial nature of task assignments under precedence and capacity 
constraints, the SPSP is classified as strongly NP-Hard \citep{blazewicz1983, kolisch1999}. 
A comprehensive review of SPSP, detailing the different approaches and resolution 
methods explored in the literature, can be found in the survey by \citet{vegavelazquez2018}.

Mathematical models for SPSP often differentiate teams strictly by their 
technical skills \citep{alba2007, chicano2011, pritsker1969}. This simplification 
does not account for cognitive and behavioral factors inherent to software 
engineering \citep{demarco1987}. Furthermore, the non-linearity of productivity 
in software projects is documented in empirical literature. \citet{brooks1975} 
formulated Brooks's Law, which postulates that adding manpower to a late 
software project tends to make it even later, due to the exponential growth 
in communication \textit{overhead}\footnote{Refers to the time and effort 
consumed in exchanging information and aligning among team members. As the 
number of members in the same activity increases, the complexity of communication 
lines grows exponentially, generating progressive losses in net productivity.} 
and the restrictive topology of precedences. Although a recurring concept in 
agile management, this dynamic is rarely structured as an endogenous variable 
in analytical MILP models \citep{ryan2013, acuna2006}.

This paper aims to represent behavioral alignment through a numerical component 
within a mathematical optimization model. Using the Solides psychometric 
methodology, which categorizes professionals into Executors, Communicators, 
Planners, and Analysts \citep{costa2018}, the model adjusts a task's nominal 
execution time based on the psychological compatibility and technical surplus 
of the individual. The study integrates behavioral profiles into an SPSP 
formulation, evaluates a cubic polynomial penalty function, analyzes the 
mathematical effects of Brooks's Law, and explores tactical capacity planning 
through a heatmap.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 details the 
mathematical modeling and the time adjustment function. Section 3 describes 
the experimental methodology and the generation of computational instances. 
Section 4 presents the results obtained by the optimizer and the managerial 
analyses. Finally, Section 5 concludes the work and proposes guidelines for 
future research.

\section{Mathematical Modeling}

The central premise of this model is that technical productivity is impacted 
by the developer's profile. The proposed MILP formulation calculates an adjusted 
execution time \textit{a priori} for each task-developer pair based on combined 
technical and behavioral characteristics.

\subsection{Sets, Parameters, and Variables}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent\textbf{Sets and Indices}
\begin{center}
    \begin{tabular}{p{2.5cm} p{12cm}}

        $S_{max}$ & Set of available \textit{sprints}, indexed by $s \in \{1, \dots, |S_{max}|\}$. \\
        $H$ & Set of technical skills, indexed by $h \in \{1, \dots, |H|\}$. \\
        $P$ & Set of the 4 behavioral quadrants, indexed by $p \in \{1, \dots, 4\}$.
    \end{tabular}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent\textbf{Parameters}
\begin{center}
    \begin{tabular}{p{2.5cm} p{12cm}}
        $Ht_{jh}$ & Minimum technical level $h$ required by task $j$. \\
        $Hd_{ih}$ & Current technical level of developer $i$ in skill $h$. \\
        $Pt_{jp}$ & Fraction of behavioral profile $p$ required for task $j$. \\
        $Pd_{ip}$ & Fraction of behavioral profile $p$ of developer $i$. \\
        $T^{base}_j$ & Estimated base time to complete task $j$ (hours). \\
        $Cap_{is}$ & Available capacity (hours) of developer $i$ in \textit{sprint} $s$. \\
        $\delta_s$ & Duration of each \textit{sprint} in calendar days. \\
        $j_{lab}$ & Usable working hours per day. \\
        $Pred(j)$ & Set of tasks that strictly precede task $j$.
    \end{tabular}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent\textbf{Decision Variables}
\begin{center}
    \begin{tabular}{p{2.5cm} p{12cm}}
        $x_{ijs} \in \{0, 1\}$ & 1 if task $j$ is assigned to developer $i$ in \textit{sprint} $s$, 0 otherwise. \\
        $y_s \in \{0, 1\}$ & 1 if \textit{sprint} $s$ is used, 0 otherwise. \\
        $TF_s \ge 0$ & Continuous variable of the accumulated final time (days) in \textit{sprint} $s$. \\
        $T^{max} \ge 0$ & Continuous variable representing the project's \textit{Makespan}.
    \end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{Dynamic Time Calculation \texorpdfstring{($T_{ij}$)}{Tij}}

The numerical adjustment of the execution time is structured in three stages 
that quantify the impact of human variables on productivity:

\vspace{0.2cm}
\noindent\textbf{Stage 1: Technical Surplus ($ET_{ij}$)} \\
Evaluates the surplus technical qualification of the assigned resource. If the 
developer exceeds the minimum stipulated requirement, task execution is accelerated. 
A scalar factor of $0.5$ is incorporated into the equation as a damping 
coefficient, smoothing the productivity gain to be more consistent with 
practical reality:
\begin{equation}
    ET_{ij} = 0.5 \cdot \left[ \frac{1}{|H|} \sum_{h \in H} \left(\max(0, Hd_{ih} - Ht_{jh})\right)^2 \right] \quad \forall i \in M, \forall j \in N
\end{equation}

\vspace{0.2cm}
\noindent\textbf{Stage 2: Behavioral Error ($EC_{ij}$)} \\
To quantify the cognitive wear caused by psychological misalignment, the absolute 
difference between the quadrants of the behavioral profile required by the task 
and the developer's profile, denoted by $\psi_{ij}$, is initially calculated. 
The division by $2$ acts as a normalization factor: since behavioral profiles 
are represented by proportional distributions (whose vector sum is always $1.0$), 
the maximum possible absolute difference between two diametrically opposed 
profiles results in $2.0$. This division ensures that the metric $\psi_{ij}$ 
strictly converges to the continuous interval of $[0, 1]$. Next, a polynomial 
function of order $n$ with a gain multiplier $k$ is applied to penalize more 
severe misalignments. These parameters were empirically defined as $n=3$ and 
$k=5.0$, a formulation whose justification is detailed in Section 4.1:
\begin{equation}
    \psi_{ij} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{p \in P} |Pd_{ip} - Pt_{jp}| \quad \forall i \in M, \forall j \in N
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    EC_{ij} = 5.0 \cdot (\psi_{ij})^3 \quad \forall i \in M, \forall j \in N
\end{equation}

\vspace{0.2cm}
\noindent\textbf{Stage 3: Adjusted Execution Time ($T_{ij}$)} \\
The final composition consolidates the simultaneous interaction of variables 
over the nominal time, assigning the efficiency gain provided by technical 
qualification and the penalty generated by behavioral inadequacy:
\begin{equation}
    T_{ij} = T^{base}_j \times (1.0 + EC_{ij} - ET_{ij}) \quad \forall i \in M, \forall j \in N
\end{equation}

\subsection{Objective Function and Constraints}

The model minimizes the final delivery schedule of the project, represented by 
the \textit{makespan}:
\begin{equation}
    \min T^{max}
\end{equation}

The task allocation is subject to the operational and logical constraints 
described below. The first constraint ensures that each task is processed 
exactly once and assigned to a single developer in the project cycle:
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{i \in M} \sum_{s \in S_{max}} x_{ijs} = 1 \quad \forall j \in N
\end{equation}

The qualification constraint prevents assignment if the designated resource does 
not possess the minimum technical knowledge required by the task in all skills:
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{i \in M} \sum_{s \in S_{max}} Hd_{ih} \cdot x_{ijs} \ge Ht_{jh} \quad \forall j \in N, \forall h \in H
\end{equation}

The workload is limited by the capacity constraint, which restricts the total 
hours worked by a developer to their available limit in the respective \textit{sprint}:
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{j \in N} T_{ij} \cdot x_{ijs} \le Cap_{is} \quad \forall i \in M, \forall s \in S_{max}
\end{equation}

To preserve the code topology, the structural precedence constraint forces 
successor tasks to be scheduled in \textit{sprints} strictly after their 
predecessors:
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{i \in M} \sum_{s \in S_{max}} s \cdot x_{ijs} \ge \sum_{i \in M} \sum_{s \in S_{max}} s \cdot x_{iks} + 1 \quad \forall j \in N, \forall k \in Pred(j)
\end{equation}

The \textit{sprint} activation logic ensures that tasks are only allocated in 
active cycles and prevents the emergence of inactive gaps (holes) in the 
chronological planning:
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{i \in M}\sum_{j \in N} x_{ijs} \ge y_s \quad \forall s \in S_{max}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    x_{ijs} \le y_s \quad \forall i \in M, \forall j \in N, \forall s \in S_{max}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    y_{s-1} \ge y_s \quad \forall s \in S_{max} \setminus \{1\}
\end{equation}

The allocated effort hours are converted into calendar days, structuring the 
continuous temporal progression of work throughout the \textit{sprint}:
\begin{equation}
    \delta_s \cdot (s-1) \cdot y_s + \frac{1}{j_{lab}} \sum_{j \in N} x_{ijs} \cdot T_{ij} \le TF_s \quad \forall i \in M, \forall s \in S_{max}
\end{equation}

Finally, the main variable of interest of the objective function is formalized, 
ensuring that the \textit{makespan} encompasses the completion time of the 
last used \textit{sprint}:
\begin{equation}
    TF_s \le T^{max} \quad \forall s \in S_{max}
\end{equation}

\section{Experimental Methodology}

To guarantee statistical reliability, a Python generator instantiated the input 
matrices pseudo-randomly. The dataset comprises 1380 instances, ensuring 30 
samples per scenario, with nominal times ($T^{base}_j$) projected for deadlines 
of 30 to 100 calendar days. 

In all experiments, the team size ($M$) was restricted to the interval of 3 
to 8 members. This range reflects the real standard for agile \textit{squads}, 
given that the literature demonstrates that teams with more than 9 members 
suffer drastic productivity drops due to excessive communication and 
coordination \cite{kniberg2012, schwaber2017}. The instances were categorized 
into five groups:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{MILP Scalability:} Examines the computational effort and 
    combinatorial growth of the optimization model by progressively sizing the 
    task \textit{backlog} ($N \in [10, 80]$), keeping the team fixed at the 
    median value of $M = 5$.
    
    \item \textbf{Behavioral Noise:} Evaluates the impact on the deadline under 
    four allocations: \textit{Baseline}, Natural, Critical, and Extreme.
    
    \item \textbf{Brooks's Law:} Analyzes tactical capacity planning by 
    iterating the task volume ($N \in [25, 60]$) and the team size 
    ($M \in [3, 8]$).

    \item \textbf{Precedence Density:} Quantifies how the proportion of 
    interdependence between tasks impacts the total \textit{makespan}, 
    identifying the saturation point where the project becomes infeasible 
    within the \textit{sprint} budget.

    \item \textbf{Technical Surplus:} Evaluates the productivity gains of 
    highly qualified teams in contrast to behavioral noise, identifying the 
    break-even point between seniority and psychological alignment.
\end{itemize}

The formulations were processed through the Gurobi Optimizer, executed in a 
computational environment equipped with a 13th Gen Intel Core i7-1360P 
processor (2.20 GHz) and 16 GB of RAM. In order to provide a balanced trade-off 
between convergence quality and response time in instances with more robust 
dimensionality, the global maximum time limit was set to 3600 seconds (one 
hour), with a relative optimality tolerance (MIP Gap) established at 0.1\%.

To ensure full reproducibility of the experiments and methodological 
transparency, the complete Python source code (including the MILP modeling, 
the instance generator, and the visual analysis scripts), as well as the raw 
output data, are publicly available in the project's official repository, 
accessible through the link: \url{https://github.com/rob1913r/behavioral-spsp}.

\section{Results and Discussion}

This section presents the computational results derived from solving the 
instances in the optimizer. The discussion begins with the evaluation of the 
theoretical basis of the adopted penalty function and the computational 
efficiency of the model across different task volumes. Following this, the 
data is explored to quantify the impact of behavioral noise on the 
\textit{makespan}, mathematically validate the capacity constraints imposed 
by Brooks's Law, and investigate the empirical compensation relationship 
between technical surplus and psychological alignment.

\subsection{Theoretical Modeling of the Behavioral Penalty}

The choice of the cubic function for the Behavioral Error was based on the 
progressive analysis of misalignment. Figure \ref{fig:g01_just} divides the 
error domain $\psi \in [0, 1]$ into the Acceptance (0.0 to 0.3), Critical 
(0.3 to 0.7), and Extreme (0.7 to 1.0) zones. It was noted that linear or 
quadratic functions punish small deviations excessively, while quartic 
functions underestimate moderate friction. The cubic function proved to be 
more realistic: it absorbs mild divergences and imposes steep penalties 
only upon reaching the critical range. 

With the empirical multiplier $k=5.0$ (Figure \ref{fig:g02_calib}), the model 
reflects two realities: in the critical zone, time suffers severe inflations 
(up to $3\times$ the nominal value), representing real bottlenecks in 
communication and rework. In the extreme zone, the penalty reaches the 
practical infeasibility threshold ($6\times$), mirroring the breaking point 
where the task exceeds the \textit{sprint's} capacity limit and forces the 
mathematical rejection of the assignment.

\subsection{Evolution under the Effect of Behavioral Noise}

The \textit{Baseline} scenario acts as a mathematical lower bound of time for 
the schedule. It represents an ``ideal team'', characterized by an abstraction 
where the developers' profiles are perfectly identical to the tasks' requirements 
($Pd_{ip} = Pt_{jp}$), nullifying the behavioral error. According to Figure 
\ref{fig:g07_noise}, the transition from this scenario to the \textit{Natural} 
one (stochastic profiles) resulted in equivalent delivery deadlines. This 
occurs because the cubic penalty function absorbs subtle deviations within the 
Acceptance Zone ($\psi_{ij} \le 0.3$). Consequently, the MILP algorithm manages 
to distribute the tasks so adherently that it nullifies the inherent noise of 
a real team, matching the efficiency of a perfect scenario.

However, there is a practical limit to this combinatorial compensation. In the 
\textit{Critical} scenario, the scarcity of sufficiently compatible profiles 
removes the optimizer's evasion routes, inflating the \textit{makespan} from 
an average of 28 to over 40 days. This degradation reaches its peak in the 
\textit{Extreme} scenario, where allocations are forced to diametrically opposed 
profiles and deadlines nearly double, approaching 80 days (Figure 
\ref{fig:g08_noise}). At this level, the incompatibility raises the task time 
($T_{ij}$) so much that it exceeds the \textit{sprint's} nominal hours limit. 
In order for the solver not to declare the project infeasible, the temporary 
relaxation of the capacity constraint ($Cap_{is}$) becomes essential.

The growth of the Behavioral Error ($EC$) responsible for this impact is 
detailed in Figure \ref{fig:g09_noise}. The presence of the standard deviation 
bars validates the methodological design: they exist in the \textit{Baseline} 
and \textit{Natural} scenarios due to the stochastic generation (Dirichlet 
distribution), which simulates the organic variability of the teams. In 
contrast, these bars are null in the \textit{Critical} and \textit{Extreme} 
scenarios, as deterministic and fixed profiles were employed in these to 
isolate and quantify the maximum impact of friction without the interference 
of randomness.

\subsection{Tactical Capacity Planning and Brooks's Law}

From a managerial perspective, the model acts as a direct decision-support 
tool. The tactical heatmap (Figure \ref{fig:g10_brooks}) allows leaders to 
quickly evaluate the cost-benefit of resizing the team. In the case of $N=50$ 
tasks, for example, expanding the team from 3 to 4 developers reduces the 
\textit{makespan} from 92 to 66 days (a drop of $\approx 28\%$). On the other 
hand, jumping from 6 to 8 professionals brings a lower marginal return, 
reducing the deadline from 44 to 32 days ($\approx 27\%$ accumulated in the 
leap of two developers).

This exhaustion of efficiency, isolated in Figure \ref{fig:g11_brooks} for the 
demand of $N = 50$ tasks, evidences Brooks's Law quantitatively. The law 
postulates that adding manpower to a project hits limits of parallelism and 
increased communication costs. The algorithm replicated this empirical 
phenomenon: from a certain team size, the time curve becomes an asymptote. 
This indicates to managers that project dependencies create a ceiling where 
adding more people stops generating significant gains in delivery speed.

\subsection{Parallelism Limitations due to Precedence Density}

The drop in efficiency observed in larger teams is directly linked to how the 
tasks in the backlog are interconnected. Graphs \ref{fig:g12_prec} and 
\ref{fig:g13_prec} show that excessive links and dependencies between 
activities limit the optimizer's ability to organize allocations in parallel.

When the proportion of precedence relations exceeds the 30\% threshold of 
possible connections, delivery times begin to rise rapidly. More critical 
than the isolated delay is the saturation point identified in Graph 
\ref{fig:g13_prec}. At this level of interdependence, the model reaches a 
limit where the volume of tasks can no longer be fitted within the available 
\textit{sprint} budget, making the project mathematically infeasible. This 
scenario reinforces the current trend of replacing monolithic code structures 
with microservices. By reducing direct dependencies, the mathematical model 
is allowed to find windows of parallelism, ensuring delivery within the schedule.

\subsection{Trade-off between Technical Proficiency and Behavioral Alignment}

The final stage of the analysis explores the trade-off relationship between 
technical productivity gain ($ET$) and losses generated by behavioral 
misalignment ($EC$). Figure \ref{fig:g14_surplus} confirms the traditional 
behavior expected in the literature: when we maintain the team with a stable 
psychological profile, increasing technical seniority (higher $ET$) results 
in consistent reductions in the \textit{makespan}.

However, the main contribution of this model lies in the juxtaposition of 
these two metrics, revealed in Figure \ref{fig:g15_paradoxo}. The mapping of 
the intersection point of the curves demonstrates an important result: a Junior 
developer ($ET = 0.0$) suited to the behavioral requirements of the task will 
be able to execute it in less time than a Senior developer ($ET = 1.0$) 
operating in the Critical or Extreme error zones. This scenario suggests that 
high technical qualification alone is not enough to compensate for the delays 
generated by a strong incompatibility of profiles in the team. Consequently, 
\textit{squad} formation practices that incorporate psychological mapping cease 
to be merely a human resources differential and come to mean a real possibility 
of generating greater operational efficiency.

\section{Conclusions and Future Work}

This paper presented a mathematical modeling for the Software Project 
Scheduling Problem (SPSP) that seeks to complement traditional approaches by 
incorporating not only technical constraints but also behavioral variables. 
Through a MILP model and the calibration of a cubic penalty function, the 
results indicate that integrating the psychological compatibility of the team 
into task allocation contributes to the generation of more accurate and 
realistic schedules.

The computational experiments generated quantitative results applicable to 
tactical project management. First, it was demonstrated that planning blind 
to the human profile presents significant deviations, with the Extreme 
friction scenario inflating the \textit{makespan} to levels that surpass the 
structural capacity of the \textit{sprints}. Second, the model mathematically 
simulated Brooks's Law, identifying the saturation point where increasing the 
team stops accelerating deliveries. Finally, a relevant socio-technical 
dynamic was observed: a Junior developer aligned with the task's profile can 
finish it in less time than a Senior subjected to a high level of cognitive noise.

As guidelines for future research, the expansion of the mathematical model is 
proposed to cover more complex operational dynamics. Among the mapped 
improvements, topological flexibilization stands out to allow the splitting 
of extensive tasks across multiple \textit{sprints} and the refinement of 
precedence constraints, enabling the start of dependent activities within the 
same agile cycle. Furthermore, it is planned to introduce individual parallelism 
(one developer working on multiple simultaneous tasks) and the allocation of 
small teams for a single activity. In the latter case, the modeling should 
incorporate variables to quantify communication \textit{overhead}, the 
adaptation curve\footnote{Refers to the initial time and cognitive effort 
required for the team to understand the context, code architecture, and specific 
requirements of a task before reaching their full productivity.}, and intra-team 
behavioral synergy\footnote{A theoretical metric that evaluates how the 
combination of different psychological profiles allocated to the same activity 
affects collaboration, problem-solving dynamics, and the group's joint efficiency.}, 
ensuring that the addition of new members complements the work cell and does 
not intensify cognitive friction.
